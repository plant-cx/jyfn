Electromagnetism

Electromagnetism is one of the four fundamental forces of nature and it's responsible for almost everything you interact with daily. It's the force that holds atoms together, makes magnets stick to your fridge, lets electricity flow through wires, and allows light to travel through space. At its core, electromagnetism describes how electrically charged particles interact with each other through electric and magnetic fields.

Electric Fields and Forces

Electric fields are created by charged particles and they exert forces on other charged particles nearby. There are two types of electric charge: positive and negative. The basic rule is simple but super important - opposite charges attract each other while like charges repel each other. So a positive charge and a negative charge will pull toward each other, but two positive charges or two negative charges will push each other away.

Charges in Action


Protons carry positive charge, electrons carry negative charge, and neutrons have no charge at all. When you rub a balloon on your hair and it sticks to the wall, that's because you've transferred electrons and created an electric field. The strength of an electric field depends on how much charge is creating it and how far away you are from that charge. The closer you get, the stronger the force becomes, following an inverse square law.

Magnetic Fields and Motion

Magnetic fields are created by moving electric charges, which means electricity and magnetism are deeply connected. When electrons flow through a wire, they create a magnetic field around that wire. This is how electromagnets work - you can turn magnetism on and off by controlling the electric current. Permanent magnets have magnetic fields because their electrons are all spinning in the same direction, creating a constant field.

Magnetic Poles


Every magnet has two poles called north and south, and just like electric charges, opposite poles attract while like poles repel. But here's something cool - you can never have just a north pole or just a south pole by itself. If you break a magnet in half, you don't get separate north and south pieces, you get two smaller magnets each with their own north and south poles. Moving charges in a magnetic field experience a force perpendicular to both their motion and the field direction, which is how electric motors work.

Maxwell's Equations

James Clerk Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism into four elegant equations that describe all electromagnetic phenomena. The first equation says that electric charges create electric fields that spread outward from positive charges and inward toward negative charges. The second equation states that magnetic monopoles don't exist - magnetic field lines always form closed loops.

Unifying Light and Electromagnetism


The third equation, Faraday's Law, explains that a changing magnetic field creates an electric field, which is how generators produce electricity. The fourth equation, Ampere's Law with Maxwell's addition, shows that electric currents and changing electric fields create magnetic fields. Together, these equations revealed something amazing - they predicted that changing electric and magnetic fields could create waves that travel through space at a specific speed, and that speed turned out to be exactly the speed of light. This proved that light itself is an electromagnetic wave, unifying optics with electricity and magnetism into one complete theory.
