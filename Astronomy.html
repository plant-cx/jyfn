Astronomy

Astronomy is the study of everything beyond Earth's atmosphere - stars, planets, galaxies, and the universe itself. Its basically trying to understand how the cosmos works on the largest scales imaginable. From understanding how stars are born and die to figuring out the expansion of the universe, astronomy combines physics with observation to answer some of the biggest questions humans have ever asked.

Redshift and the Expanding Universe

Redshift is one of the most important discoveries in astronomy. When objects in space move away from us the light they emit gets stretched to longer wavelengths, making it appear more red. This is called the Doppler effect for light. Edwin Hubble discovered that basically all galaxies are moving away from us, and the farther away they are, the faster they're moving.

The Big Bang Evidence


This redshift proved the universe is expanding, which led to the Big Bang theory. If everything is moving apart now then in the past everything must have been closer together. By measuring redshift astronomers can figure out how fast galaxies are moving and how far away they are. The amount of redshift also tells us about the age of the universe which is around 13.8 billion years old.

Birth of a Star

Stars start their lives in huge clouds of gas and dust called nebulae. Gravity pulls this material together and as it collapses it heats up. When the core gets hot enough - around 10 million degrees - nuclear fusion starts. Hydrogen atoms fuse together to make helium and this releases massive amounts of energy. Thats what makes stars shine.

Main Sequence and Beyond


For most of a stars life it sits in what's called the main sequence, where it steadily fuses hydrogen into helium. Our Sun has been doing this for about 4.6 billion years and will keep going for another 5 billion. The more massive a star is the faster it burns through its fuel. Really massive stars might only last a few million years while tiny red dwarfs can burn for trillions of years.

Death of a Star

When a star runs out of hydrogen in its core it starts fusing heavier elements. Smaller stars like our Sun will expand into red giants, then shed their outer layers to form planetary nebulae, leaving behind a white dwarf. Massive stars go through more fusion stages creating elements all the way up to iron. But iron fusion doesnt release energy - it absorbs it. So the core collapses suddenly causing a supernova explosion.

Black Holes and Neutron Stars


After a supernova the core of a massive star can collapse into either a neutron star or a black hole. Neutron stars are incredibly dense - a teaspoon would weigh billions of tons. Black holes are even more extreme, their gravity is so strong that not even light can escape. The boundary around a black hole where this happens is called the event horizon. Once something crosses it theres no coming back.

Dark Matter and Dark Energy

Heres where things get weird. About 95% of the universe is made of stuff we cant see. Dark matter makes up about 27% and we know its there because of its gravitational effects on galaxies. Galaxies rotate way too fast to be held together by just the matter we can see. Dark energy is even more mysterious - it makes up about 68% of the universe and its causing the expansion to accelerate which honestly shouldn't be happening.

Gravitational Waves


Einstein predicted that massive objects moving through space create ripples in spacetime itself. In 2015 scientists finally detected gravitational waves for the first time using LIGO. They came from two black holes colliding over a billion light-years away. This opened up a completley new way of studying the universe - instead of looking at light we can now listen to the vibrations of spacetime.
