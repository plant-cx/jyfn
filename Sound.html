Sound Physics

Sound is a mechanical wave that travels through matter by causing particles to vibrate back and forth. When you speak, your vocal cords create vibrations in the air molecules around them. These vibrations spread outward in all directions, creating regions of compressed and stretched air that your ears detect as sound. Unlike light, sound cannot travel through empty space because it needs matter to carry the vibrations from place to place.

How Sound Waves Work

Sound waves are longitudinal waves, meaning particles vibrate parallel to the direction the wave travels. Imagine pushing and pulling a slinky - the coils bunch up and spread out, creating compressions and rarefactions that travel along its length. In air, molecules get squeezed together (compression) and spread apart (rarefaction) as the wave passes. The speed of sound depends on the medium - it travels about 343 meters per second in air at room temperature, but much faster in water (1500 m/s) and even faster in steel (5000 m/s).

Pitch and Volume


Pitch is determined by frequency - how many waves pass a point each second, measured in hertz (Hz). Higher frequencies create higher pitched sounds. Humans can hear from about 20 Hz (very low bass) to 20,000 Hz (very high treble). Volume depends on amplitude - how much the air molecules are displaced from their resting position. Larger vibrations create louder sounds. The decibel scale measures volume logarithmically: normal conversation is around 60 dB, while rock concerts can exceed 110 dB, loud enough to cause permanent hearing damage.

Wave Behavior and Effects

Sound waves exhibit several important behaviors. Reflection creates echoes when waves bounce off surfaces - this is how bats navigate and how we use sonar. Refraction bends sound waves when they pass through different materials, which is why voices sound different underwater. Interference occurs when waves meet - constructive interference makes sounds louder while destructive interference can cancel sounds out (used in noise-canceling headphones). The Doppler effect changes pitch when the source or listener moves, explaining why ambulance sirens change pitch as they pass by.

Key Facts

01

What is sound?

Mechanical waves that travel through matter by causing particles to vibrate back and forth in compressions and rarefactions.

02

How do waves move?

Longitudinal waves where particles vibrate parallel to wave direction, traveling faster through denser materials.

03

What creates pitch and volume?

Frequency determines pitch (20-20,000 Hz for humans), while amplitude controls volume measured in decibels.

04

How do waves behave?

Sound reflects (echoes), refracts (bends), interferes (combines), and shows Doppler effect (pitch changes with motion).
