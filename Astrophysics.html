Astrophysics

Astrophysics applies the laws of physics to understand celestial objects and phenomena beyond Earth. It explains how stars form from collapsing gas clouds, why they shine for billions of years, and how they eventually die in spectacular explosions or collapse into black holes. By studying light from distant galaxies, we learn about the universe's history, structure, and ultimate fate. Astrophysics reveals that the same physical laws governing a falling apple also control the motion of planets, the fusion reactions powering stars, and the expansion of space itself.

Stellar Physics

Stars are massive nuclear fusion reactors held together by gravity. In their cores, hydrogen nuclei overcome electromagnetic repulsion to fuse into helium, releasing enormous amounts of energy as light and heat. This process maintains a delicate balance - gravity tries to collapse the star while radiation pressure pushes outward. A star's mass determines its entire life story: low-mass stars like our Sun burn steadily for 10 billion years, while massive stars live fast and die young in supernova explosions that forge heavy elements and scatter them across space.

The Expanding Universe


The universe began 13.8 billion years ago in the Big Bang and has been expanding ever since. This expansion was discovered by observing that distant galaxies show redshift - their light is stretched to longer wavelengths as space itself expands. The universe contains ordinary matter (5%), dark matter (27%), and dark energy (68%). Dark matter provides the gravitational scaffolding for galaxy formation, while dark energy accelerates the expansion. The cosmic microwave background radiation gives us a snapshot of the universe when it was only 380,000 years old and first became transparent to light.
