Electricity

Electricity is the flow of electric charge and its literally what powers modern civilization. From the lights in your room to your phone to massive power grids, understanding electricity is essential for A-level physics. It involves current, voltage, resistance and the various laws that govern how circuits behave.

Conventional vs Electron Flow

Heres something confusing that trips everyone up at first. Current is defined as flowing from positive to negative - this is called conventional current. But in reality electrons actually flow from negative to positive because they're negatively charged and get attracted to the positive terminal. This is called electron flow.

Why the Confusion?


The reason for this weirdness is historical. Benjamin Franklin defined current flow before anyone knew about electrons so he just guessed that positive charges were moving. By the time scientists discovered electrons and realized they were negative it was too late to change all the conventions. So we still use conventional current in circuit diagrams even though we know its actually electrons moving the opposite way.

Series Circuits

In a series circuit all components are connected in a single loop. The current is the same everywhere in the circuit because theres only one path for it to follow. The voltage across each component adds up to equal the total voltage from the battery. If you add more resistors the total resistance increases because current has to flow through all of them. If one component breaks the whole circuit stops working.

Parallel Circuits


In a parallel circuit components are connected across multiple paths. The voltage across each branch is the same as the battery voltage. But the current splits up between the different branches depending on their resistance. The total resistance actually decreases when you add more parallel branches because you're giving current more paths to flow through. If one branch breaks the others keep working which is why house wiring uses parallel circuits.

Ohm's Law

Ohms Law is probably the most important equation in electricity. It says V = IR which means voltage equals current times resistance. This tells you that if you increase the voltage the current goes up proportionally. If you increase the resistance the current goes down. Its really simple but super powerful for analyzing circuits. Just remember it only works for ohmic conductors - materials where resistance stays constant.

Kirchhoff's Laws


Kirchhoffs Current Law says that current going into a junction equals current coming out. This is basically conservation of charge - electrons cant just disappear. Kirchhoffs Voltage Law says that the sum of voltages around any closed loop equals zero. This comes from conservation of energy. These laws let you analyze really complicated circuits by breaking them down into simpler parts. You'll use them constantly in A-level problems.

Power and Energy

Power is the rate of energy transfer and in circuits its given by P = IV which is power equals current times voltage. You can also use P = I²R or P = V²/R depending on what you know. This tells you how much energy per second is being converted - usually to heat in a resistor. Understanding power is crucial for designing circuits that dont overheat and for calculating electricity costs.

Internal Resistence


Real batteries aren't perfect - they have internal resistance which means they cant deliver all their voltage to the circuit. The terminal voltage you actually get is less than the EMF because some voltage is lost across this internal resistance. The equation is V = E - Ir where E is the EMF, V is terminal voltage, I is current and r is internal resistance. This is why batteries get warm when you use them and why they perform worse when delivering high currents.
